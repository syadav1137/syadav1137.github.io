THE MESSAGE

A Story About the Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

Follow mRNA on its journey from birth inside the nucleus to delivering a message that builds life itself.

Written and Illustrated · Sunny Yadav · 2025

Page 1: The Keeper of Instructions

Deep inside every living cell, locked in a place called the NUCLEUS...
DNA
I am DNA. Inside me lives every instruction this cell will ever need. Every protein it will ever build. Every function it will ever carry out.
I have been here since this cell was born. I will be here until it divides.
But I never leave this nucleus. So how does my message reach the rest of the cell...?
What is DNA? DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double-stranded molecule shaped like a twisted ladder. The rungs are made of paired chemical bases: A pairs with T, and G pairs with C. The sequence of these bases spells out the genetic code, the instructions for building proteins.

Page 2: Enter RNA Polymerase

DNA is not alone in the nucleus.
RNA Polymerase
Name is RNA Polymerase. I build things. That is my job.
DNA tells me where to start. I do the rest.

RNA Polymerase: the most dependable worker in the nucleus. Does not say much. Gets things done.

RNA Polymerase gets to work.

RNA Polymerase finds a promoter on DNA, a signal that says "start here." It grabs the DNA and unzips the two strands.

UNZIP!
One base at a time. Matching the template. This is how you build a message.
TRANSCRIPTION RNA Polymerase reads one strand of DNA and builds a complementary RNA molecule. Instead of T, RNA uses U (uracil). The result is a single-stranded mRNA with the same sequence as the coding strand of DNA, but with U in place of T.

Page 3: A Messenger is Born

The mRNA is complete.
mRNA
I exist. I have a message inside me. I can feel it.
What does it say? Where am I supposed to go?

mRNA: freshly made, full of purpose, and completely new to the world.

RNA Polymerase gives a rare speech.
RNA Polymerase
You carry a copy of DNA's instructions. Not the whole thing, just one gene. One message.
Your job is to get out of this nucleus and find a ribosome. They will know what to do with you.
Do not dawdle.
Right. Out of the nucleus. Find a ribosome. Got it.

Page 4: The Gatekeeper

The edge of the nucleus...
Nuclear Pore
HALT. Name and purpose.
mRNA presents itself.
mRNA! Carrying a gene from DNA. Need to get to the cytoplasm. Ribosome business.
Cap structure present. Poly-A tail in order. Export signals verified.
You may pass. Move along.
Processed mRNA Before leaving the nucleus, mRNA gets a protective cap, a poly-A tail, and has non-coding introns removed. Only then can it pass through the nuclear pore.
mRNA exits the nucleus.
WHOOSH!
The cytoplasm. It is so big. And busy. Where is that ribosome?

Page 5: Lost in the Cytoplasm

mRNA wanders.
The cytoplasm is enormous. There are so many things in here...

The cytoplasm is crowded with organelles, proteins, and molecules all moving at once.

mRNA bumps into things.
Excuse me, coming through. Power generation happening here.
Sorry! I am looking for a ribosome...?
Ribosomes are everywhere. Keep moving.
And then mRNA sees it.
THERE! A RIBOSOME!
Finding the ribosome mRNA has a special sequence called the 5' UTR that helps ribosomes recognize and bind it. The ribosome finds the mRNA.

Page 6: The Ribosome

The Ribosome introduces itself.
Ribosome
Ah, an mRNA. Welcome. I have been doing this for a very long time.
Come, thread yourself through. I will read your message and build what it says.
That is what I do. That is all I do. And I love it.
You can read it? What does it say?
We will find out together. First: let us find your beginning.
THE START CODON.
AUG: Start Codon
ATTENTION! THE MESSAGE BEGINS HERE! AUG! THE FIRST CODON! METHIONINE FIRST!
Every time. So dramatic.
The Start Codon AUG is the start codon. It tells the ribosome where translation begins and codes for the amino acid methionine. Almost every protein starts with methionine.

Page 7: The Delivery Crew

Meet the tRNAs.

The ribosome reads the mRNA codon by codon. For each codon, a matching tRNA arrives carrying one amino acid. The tRNAs are small, fast, and very proud of their cargo.

tRNA
tRNA
tRNA
tRNA
ALANINE DELIVERY! Codon CGU, present!
VALINE HERE! Codon CAC, ready!
One at a time. Match your anticodon, drop your amino acid, and make way for the next.
tRNA and the Genetic Code Each tRNA has an anticodon (3 bases matching the mRNA codon) and an attached amino acid. There are 64 possible codons but only 20 amino acids, so some amino acids are coded by multiple codons. This is called the degeneracy of the genetic code.

Page 8: The Chain Grows

CLICK. CLICK. CLICK.

One by one, tRNAs arrive, match their codon, drop their amino acid, and leave. The amino acids link together with peptide bonds, forming a growing chain.

Each amino acid is added precisely. The sequence of the protein is determined entirely by the sequence of the mRNA. Which was determined by the DNA.
So I am not just a messenger. I am the blueprint.
Yes. Exactly.
THE STOP CODON arrives.
Stop Codon
The message is complete. It is time to stop.
This is not sad. This is the point. Every message has an end.
Release factor incoming. Protein chain released.
Stop Codons There are three stop codons: UAA, UAG, and UGA. None code for an amino acid. Instead they signal release factors to enter the ribosome and free the completed protein chain.

Protein, born!
My message was delivered. The chain is built. I can rest now.
Another good one. Off you go.
I AM ALIVE! I AM A PROTEIN! WHAT DO I DO NOW?!
What happens next? The newly made protein chain folds into a specific 3D shape determined by its amino acid sequence. This shape determines what the protein does, where it goes, and what it interacts with. Molecular chaperones often help guide the folding process.

Page 10: A Life of Purpose

The protein folds.
FOLD!
I am taking shape. I can feel what I am supposed to do...

The amino acid sequence determines the final 3D shape. Shape is function.

The protein has a job.

Proteins do almost everything in a cell. This one is an enzyme, a protein that speeds up chemical reactions.

I fit these two molecules perfectly. I was shaped to do exactly this.
What about mRNA?

Back at the ribosome, mRNA's job is done. The cell breaks it down. Its nucleotides will be recycled.

I lived a short life. But the protein I built will go on for a long time. And do important things.
That is enough for me.

Page 11: The Bigger Picture

Meanwhile, in the same cell, at the same moment...

This story happened once. But in a single human cell, there are roughly 100,000 ribosomes, translating thousands of different mRNAs simultaneously. One mRNA can be read by many ribosomes at once, forming a structure called a polysome. The Central Dogma is not a single event. It is a constant, thundering process.

The Central Dogma: The Full Story

From DNA to Protein: the flow of life's information.

The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology, proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, describes the flow of genetic information: DNA is transcribed into mRNA, which is translated into protein. This process happens in every living cell on Earth.

The End.

mRNA lived briefly but well. The protein it built folded into a shape the universe had never seen before, went off to do something important, and kept the cell alive for another moment. Somewhere in your body, right now, this story is happening billions of times over. It has been happening since the first cell divided, roughly 3.5 billion years ago. It will happen until the last one does.
